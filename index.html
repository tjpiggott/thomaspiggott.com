Because you need to know you're solving the right problem
People are natural problem solvers and are quick to come up with solutions. The discovery phase of any project is vital for a healthy project because it slows this natural reaction down. Observations and interviews with real people, in their real environment, helps confirms the problem and locates the real opportunity. These insights will ultimately determine the direction of the entire project and set up the designs for success.
observations & interviews
Interviews and observation are the fundamental building block to understanding the problem space and the user. So, I head out into the field with pen and notebook, ready to not just ask about what's going on, but to observe in context how users carry out their tasks. It's not unusual to find that what a user says versus what they do isn't that same thing.
personas
Personas are one of the most powerful tools for focusing design efforts where they belong: on the end user. Light weight and flexible, personas allow the business and designers to remove their biases and speak a common, user-centric language when talking about what is important for the product.
workflows
Workflows help me ensure that I know what processes have been captured and where potential information is missing. This also helps identify These lead directly into design, so it's important to know the user's underlying workflow.
Because you need pixels where they matter most
The design phase is about testing design assumptions. No design is ever perfect, but it's my job to defend each pixel based on interviews, observations, and design assessments with real users. That's why iterating designs is important, so that feedback can be continuously incorporated back into the design. This ensures that the user's voice is heard and their needs are met.
wireframes
Wireframing helps me setup my thinking after carrying out all the ux research. Often, I use pen and paper (or whiteboard) to start because of how quick and easy it is. Once the basic frame is in place, designs can be moved up in fidelity with tools such as Axure, Balsamiq, or Fireworks.
prototypes
Prototypes come in all shapes and sizes. I like to start with paper prototypes because of how quickly they can be formed. As detail increases, I like to move to Axure, Photoshop, and even custom prototypes in html / css / javascript when appropriate. Take a look at a custom HTML 5 prototype made for a tablet reading app here.
design assessments
Design Assessments are key to testing design assumptions. Getting a user in front of your designs and watching as they struggle through a design is as informational as it is humbling. Often, I will present multiple designs so that I can see different concepts in action. Then, when refining, I can pull what worked best from across the concepts.
pen & paper
This is my favorite tool to start designs with (this or a white board). Nothing's faster than drawing a picture when you need to express ideas quickly and get a basic structure down.
axure
Axure is the tool I am most comfortable creating prototypes in. Axure allows me to build prototypes that easily flex between sketchy looking designs to full fledged interactive prototypes. With my background as a developer, it is particularly fun for me to quickly set up complex prototypes when certain features call for it.
balsamiq
I am experienced with a wide range of prototyping tools and I pick up on new tools quickly. Balsamiq is one of those tools that is particularly nice when moving up from hand-drawn designs.
illustrator
Illustrator is a powerful tool that I use for most of my graphics work because of the flexibility afforded by vector-based graphics. It is also the tool I am most experienced with from the Adobe Creative Suite.
photoshop
Photoshop is the tool I use most often to create finalized production assets which often originate in Illustrator. Photoshop, as any designer can tell you, is perfect for adding the final layer of polish.
html/css/javascript
Because of my development background, I am extremely comfortable using html, css, and javascript to make customized prototypes. In fact, this site is coded with those technologies along with jQuery and tools based on it. I also have experience with PHP, Java, Objective-C, C#, and SQL, which is extremely useful when working with the development team.
Because design never ends at pixel-perfect mockups
Delivery starts with communicating the design to the developers and doesn't end until it's been seen through to the finish. That often means adjustments and clarification to the design as the developers find issues or the business makes further decisions. It's my job to ensure the whole team is aware of the previous steps and most importantly, the primary persona. Everyone on the team should be empowered to advocate for the user, not just the designers.
screenplay
Screenplays are a great tool for quickly describing the designs when handing off to development. I use screenplays as a way of showing the team see all potential pages and interactions. It is the best tool for quickly telling a story to stakeholders about what the final design is and what it allows for based on real user workflows.
storycards
Storycards with mockups are the best way to document the design in full detail. Because they capture low-level requirements, developers are able to estimate at a much higher confidence level.
regulated documentation
I've worked on FDA regulated software that had strict guidelines for documentation. So, I'm familiar with the rigorous level of detail and documentation needed for projects with government involvement.

the power of promiscuous pairing
This engaging presentation takes a short time to introduce pairing and then gets right into the activity: pairing promiscuously! The activity has people work as a pair to design a house. Then, they re-pair and continue work on a house that they have had nothing to do with at the start. This presentation is intended to show people that working with others, quickly and creatively, can help make work more fun and more productive. See the presentation here.
